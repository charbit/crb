\begin{frame}
 \frametitle{Station design}
A station consists of sensors. Typically the design depends on two parameters:
\begin{itemize}
 \item
the aperture which is defined by the radius of the smallest circle containing the station,
 \item
the locations of the sensors, which is more difficult to characterize by only one number. Two aspects are of interest:		
\begin{itemize}
 \item
the uniformity of the interdistances which could be useful in the case of loss of coherence. 
 \item
the isotropy which means that the accuracy is almost independent of the direction of arrivals.

\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
 \end{frame}
%=======================================================
%=======================================================
%=======================================================
\begin{frame}
 \frametitle{Coherence}
We consider a plane wave "not fully spatially coherent" and 2 points distant of $d$:
We assume that the loss of coherence (LOC) is given by
\begin{eqnarray*}
 \log \MSC(f) \approx -\beta f^2 \times d^2
\end{eqnarray*}
where $f$ is the frequency. $\beta$ characterize the LOC.
\end{frame}
%=======================================================
%=======================================================
%=======================================================
\begin{frame}
 \frametitle{CRB}
\begin{itemize}
 \item

Cramer-Rao bound (CRB) gives the minimum variance we can expect in the estimation of a parameter of interest (POI). To summarize we can use the product of the eigenvalues.

 \item
In our case the two POIs are the azimuth and horizontal velocity of the arrival. Then the CRB is a 2 by 2 positive matrix whose diagonal elements are the minimal variances on the 2 parameters of interest and the 3rd the correlation between them.


\item
CRB is a function of the true azimuth and the true horizontal velocity, the LOC parameter $\beta$, the SNR, the geometry of the station.

\end{itemize}
\end{frame}
